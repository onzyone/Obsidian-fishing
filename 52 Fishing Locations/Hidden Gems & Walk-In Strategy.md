## Blown Out? Go Here Instead — Clear Water Fallbacks

When main rivers (Credit, Humber, Don) are murky after heavy rain, these spring-fed and
groundwater-fed alternatives clear **1–3 days faster**. They're smaller water, so trout
(not steelhead) are the primary target — but they're actually fishing when the big rivers
are unfishable.

**Why smaller tribs clear faster:**
- Smaller drainage basin = less total runoff volume
- Spring-fed / limestone-fed = groundwater maintains baseflow and dilutes silt
- Forested or less-developed catchments = less soil disturbance per mm of rain

### Walk-In Rule for Tributaries

When fishing small tribs on road allowances:
1. Park at a rural road crossing — bridge/culvert = always public access
2. Wade or walk upstream (not downstream where other anglers likely went)
3. **Walk at least 500m before you start fishing** — 90% of pressure happens within 200m of parking
4. Small water = smaller flies/lures: #10 spinner, #12-16 wet fly, small Mepps/Rooster Tail

## The Core Insight

The **MNR Atlas of Brook Trout Streams and Rivers in Ontario (2003)** lists 1,663 confirmed brook trout streams. Most people have never heard of 90% of them. Any stream in the atlas that doesn't have a conservation area or named parking lot attached to it is likely unfished or very low pressure. The atlas data already embedded in these notes is your hidden gem map.

## Method: How to Scout a New Spot

1. **Start with the MNR Atlas** — use the small streams already listed below or pull new ones from [Yumpu](https://www.yumpu.com/en/document/view/18624669/atlas-of-brook-trout-streams-and-rivers-in-ontario-fisheries-reports)
2. **Drop the lat/long into Google Maps satellite view** — look for stream width, tree canopy (cold water indicator), road crossings
3. **Find a road allowance crossing** — in Ontario, the road allowance at a bridge/culvert is always public; you can fish up/downstream from there
4. **Verify on Fish ON-Line** — confirm species and regulations for that waterbody
5. **Summer scouting trick** — bring a stream thermometer; if a tiny creek runs noticeably cold (under 18°C) on a hot July day, it has brook trout. Wade in and look.
6. **Walk past the bridge** — most anglers stop within 100m of parking. Walk 30+ min in either direction and you'll likely have the water to yourself

### Key Public Access Types
- **Road allowance at culvert/bridge** — always legal to access water from here
- **Provincial park / nature reserve** — trail walk-in required; fishing usually permitted
- **Conservation area trail** — some require day-use fee; most allow fishing
- **Ontario Crown land** — free public access; use [Ontario Crown Land Use Atlas](https://www.ontario.ca/page/crown-land-use-policy-atlas) to confirm

## Popular Rivers — Walk Past the Crowds

On any well-known river, most anglers fish within **100–200m of the parking lot**. These sections are worth the extra walk on busy rivers:

| River | Strategy |
|---|---|
| Ganaraska | Park at Port Hope, walk north — crowds thin fast above Jocelyn St |
| Grand River | Iron Bridge (Weisenberg Rd) — barrier stops vehicles; walk in from dirt road parking |
| Humber River | Walk above Scarlett Mills / Raymore; upper Boyd CA stretch past most anglers |
| Noisy River | Walk past first pool at Dunedin; upper section inside park boundary much quieter |
| Nottawasaga | Township road allowances — research on map, walk in; far less used than Angus area |
